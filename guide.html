1. Start here: what to know before you book

Accessible cruising is one of the most manageable forms of accessible travel there is. You unpack once. The accommodations travel with you. Medical care is onboard if you need it. And cruise lines, more than any other travel supplier, have decades of experience serving travelers with disabilities.

But it only works if the planning is done right. Booking the wrong cabin category, missing the Access Department notification window, or skipping the tender-port review can make a great idea turn into a hard week. This guide walks you through all of it — in the same order you'll actually make decisions.

Our service is free — cruise lines pay our commission, not you.

2. Accessible cabin categories explained

Every major cruise ship has a limited number of accessible cabins, and the category you book matters more than almost any other accessibility decision. There are two main types:

Fully Accessible (HC, H, or similar)

These cabins are designed for travelers who use a wheelchair or scooter full-time. They feature:

  • Wider doorways (typically 32+ inches)
  • A large, open turning radius inside the cabin
  • A roll-in shower with a fold-down bench and handheld shower head
  • Grab bars throughout the bathroom
  • A raised toilet with side transfer room
  • Lowered closet rods, peepholes, thermostats, and safes
  • Visual alarm and doorbell notification (on most ships)

Ambulatory Accessible (HA or similar)

These cabins are for travelers who can walk but need some modifications — a larger bathroom, grab bars, or a transfer shower rather than a stepover tub. Useful for travelers who don't use a wheelchair full-time but have limited mobility, balance concerns, or need support with bathing.

A common mistake Travelers with folding scooters sometimes book standard cabins to save money, then find their scooter won't fit through the bathroom door. Even a folded scooter takes floor space at night. If you use a scooter at all, book the accessible category.

When to book

Accessible cabins book out faster than standard cabins on every ship — they're a small fraction of total inventory. Book as early as possible, ideally 9 to 12 months before sailing. For holiday weeks, new ships in their inaugural season, and Alaska season, book even earlier.

3. Mobility equipment, oxygen, and medical gear

You have two choices: bring your own, or rent through a cruise-approved supplier. Both work. The right one depends on your equipment, your home airport, and how long the flights are.

Renting through Special Needs at Sea

Special Needs at Sea is the only mobility and oxygen equipment supplier with direct delivery relationships with every major cruise line. Equipment is delivered to your cabin before you board and picked up after you disembark. Typical rental categories:

  • Manual and power wheelchairs
  • Mobility scooters (standard and heavy-duty, folding and non-folding)
  • Portable oxygen concentrators and liquid oxygen refills at major ports
  • CPAP distilled water delivery
  • Shower chairs, transfer benches, raised toilet seats
  • Patient lifts and hospital beds

Craystar arranges these rentals for you directly. Because we hold the SNG Certified Accessible Travel Advocate® designation, we have a direct working relationship with Special Needs at Sea.

Bringing your own equipment

You can bring your own wheelchair, scooter, or oxygen concentrator on every major cruise line. If you fly, notify the airline of mobility equipment at booking — airlines are federally required to transport it free of charge, but the logistics of gate-checking a power chair need to be arranged in advance. For POCs, bring enough batteries for at least 1.5× the longest flight leg plus any extended shore time.

4. Dietary accommodations and food allergies

Cruise lines handle severe dietary restrictions better than almost any other travel supplier. There's a kitchen on the ship, a head waiter assigned to your table, and the same guests eating the same dining rotation every night — which means once your needs are on file, they're addressed at every meal.

The restrictions cruise lines are equipped to handle

  • Severe food allergies — peanut, tree nut, shellfish, egg, soy, dairy, gluten
  • Celiac disease — gluten-free preparations with separate cookware on most lines
  • Diabetes — sugar-free desserts, carbohydrate counts on request, and diabetic-friendly menus
  • Kidney diets — low-potassium, low-phosphorus preparations
  • Kosher meals — pre-ordered frozen kosher meals (advance notice required)
  • Halal, vegan, vegetarian, low-sodium — standard accommodations on most lines
  • Feeding tube / liquid diets — case-by-case with advance medical coordination
How dietary accommodation actually works onboard Your head waiter will introduce themselves on the first night, confirm your restrictions, and review tomorrow's menu with you each evening. If a dish can't be modified safely, the kitchen will prepare an alternative. Specialty restaurants will need to be notified separately — Craystar flags this to the line so the paper trail follows you across the ship.

5. Autism, sensory, and cognitive needs

Several cruise lines have meaningful programs for travelers on the autism spectrum or with cognitive and developmental disabilities. Royal Caribbean leads this space.

Royal Caribbean's Autism Friendly program

Royal Caribbean became the first cruise line to receive Autism Friendly certification in 2014 through Autism on the Seas, and currently holds fleet-wide Silver Level designation. Programs include:

  • Sensory-friendly films (low-lit, low-volume, freedom to walk and talk)
  • Autism-friendly toy lending program
  • Pre-cruise social stories available for download
  • Autism-aware youth staff (Silver Level training fleet-wide)
  • Age-flexibility for Adventure Ocean — children may be placed with developmentally appropriate age groups rather than chronological
  • Adventure Ocean exceptions to toilet-training requirements for guests with disabilities
  • Priority check-in, boarding, and departure for families who request it
  • Complimentary Autism Channel access in staterooms on most ships

For all lines: sensory planning tips

  • Book a cabin away from high-traffic areas — mid-ship, mid-deck, away from elevators and the youth program entrance
  • Eat dinner at the early seating — quieter, less crowded, shorter wait
  • Request a muster drill accommodation if a crowded lifeboat station will be a trigger
  • Pack familiar snacks, a sensory kit, and weighted items as comfort anchors
  • Plan a "quiet day" mid-cruise — a sea day without an excursion

Cognitive and developmental considerations

Cruise lines accommodate Down syndrome, Fragile X, cerebral palsy, and other cognitive or developmental disabilities through their Access Department. Typical requests include written daily schedules delivered to your cabin, advance boarding, and flexibility around the youth program's standard age brackets.

6. Hearing and vision accommodations

For travelers with hearing loss

  • Accessible cabins include visual notification (doorbell, phone, and fire alarm)
  • Assistive listening devices available at theaters and venues on most ships
  • Closed captioning on in-room TVs and many onboard screens
  • ASL interpretation offered on some select sailings — request well in advance
  • TTY/TDD access available in most accessible cabins

For travelers with vision loss

  • Braille menus in main dining rooms on most major lines
  • Large-print menus and daily programs on request
  • Service animal welcomes on all major cruise lines — contact the line 60+ days ahead to confirm port entry requirements and onboard relief area
  • Audio description on select theater productions and in-room films on newer ships
  • Staff orientation to assist with ship navigation on arrival day
Service animal notice Every port has its own rules about animals entering the country. Some Caribbean and Mexican ports do not allow service animals off the ship. Craystar will flag which ports allow service animal disembarkation before you commit to an itinerary.

8. Shore excursions and tender ports

Once the ship docks, accessibility becomes a port-by-port question. Cruise lines label their accessible shore excursions, but not every port offers them — and one detail matters more than any other:

Tender ports

Some ports don't have a dock big enough for the ship. Passengers transfer by small boat ("tender") to reach shore. Tender transfers are extremely difficult — sometimes impossible — for wheelchair users, especially in choppy seas. Common tender ports include Grand Cayman, Belize, Half Moon Cay, CocoCay (sometimes), and several Alaska ports.

Before you commit to an itinerary Craystar pulls the port list and flags every tender port. For mobility-limited travelers, we often recommend itineraries that avoid tender ports entirely, or build in a sea day or spa day on a tender day rather than fighting a transfer.

Choosing accessible excursions

  • Book through the cruise line's own shore excursion catalog — third-party accessible tour operators exist, but only ship-booked excursions guarantee the ship will wait if you're delayed
  • Confirm what "accessible" means for each tour — walking distance, bus steps, restroom access, pace
  • Private car hires at port are often the most flexible option for full wheelchair users, arranged through Access Department
  • Beach days: not all cruise line private islands have beach wheelchairs (Royal Caribbean's CocoCay does; confirm for each line)

9. Travel insurance for accessible cruising

Cruise travel insurance matters more for accessible travelers than almost any other segment. The key coverages to look for:

Pre-existing condition waiver

Most travel insurance excludes claims related to pre-existing conditions by default. To get coverage for conditions diagnosed before booking, you must buy a policy with a pre-existing condition waiver — and these usually have to be purchased within 10 to 21 days of the initial trip deposit. Miss the window and you cannot buy it retroactively.

Cancel for any reason (CFAR)

CFAR is a premium upgrade that returns 50–75% of your trip cost if you cancel for reasons not covered by standard insurance — including a flare-up, a last-minute surgery, or simply a bad week. For high-medical-need travelers, it's often worth the extra cost.

Medical evacuation

Basic cruise insurance often includes only $10,000–$25,000 of medical evacuation coverage. For a serious event at sea that requires a helicopter evacuation, the actual bill can run $100,000+. Look for policies with at least $250,000 evacuation coverage, or a dedicated membership like MedjetAssist.

Documentation

Keep digital copies of all medications, prescriptions, medical equipment serial numbers, and physician contact information in your phone and in a shore-bag separate from the cabin. If a medical situation arises mid-cruise, your insurance claim will require these.

10. Pre-sailing checklist

Craystar handles most of this for you as your Certified Accessible Travel Advocate — but here's the full picture of what gets done and when:

9–12 months out

  • Book accessible cabin category
  • Purchase travel insurance with pre-existing condition waiver (within 10–21 days of first deposit)
  • Confirm passports valid for 6+ months after return
  • For service animals: confirm port entry requirements

3–6 months out

  • Notify Access Department of specific needs
  • Reserve equipment rentals through Special Needs at Sea
  • Confirm dietary restrictions in writing
  • Review shore excursion options and flag tender ports

30–60 days out

  • Medical clearance letter from physician (if sailing with complex conditions)
  • Prescription refills — bring enough for trip + 7 days buffer
  • Confirm flight mobility assistance if flying to port
  • Download social stories and ship maps (for sensory/cognitive preparation)

7 days out

  • Complete online check-in
  • Print accessibility confirmation emails
  • Pack medications and medical equipment in carry-on only (never checked)
  • Confirm transportation to port accommodates mobility device

11. On the ship: how a day actually unfolds

A short portrait of what an accessible cruise day looks like when the planning has been done right:

You arrive at the port. Because you've pre-reserved priority boarding, you bypass the general check-in line. Your scooter — delivered that morning by Special Needs at Sea — is waiting for you inside the terminal. You board, and your accessible cabin is already set up with grab bars, a roll-in shower, and a fold-down bench.

Your head waiter meets you at your first dinner, confirms your celiac and peanut allergy, and shows you tomorrow's menu. Every morning after, the daily program is waiting outside your door in large print. Your muster station is accessible. Your shore excursion in Cozumel is in a lift-equipped van with 90 minutes of beach time at a resort with a beach wheelchair.

You come home tired. Just not exhausted.

12. The 10 most common accessible cruise mistakes

  1. Booking a standard cabin with a scooter. Even folded, scooters need an accessible cabin.
  2. Missing the pre-existing condition waiver window. Usually 10–21 days from first deposit.
  3. Not notifying the Access Department. This is the single most important pre-sailing step.
  4. Checking medications. Always in the carry-on. Never in checked luggage.
  5. Booking an itinerary with too many tender ports. For mobility users, review every port.
  6. Booking non-ship shore excursions. Only ship-booked excursions guarantee the ship waits.
  7. Under-estimating oxygen battery life. Bring 1.5× what you think you'll need.
  8. Skipping the head waiter introduction. This is where the ship gets dietary needs right.
  9. Assuming every port allows service animals. Some Caribbean and Mexican ports do not.
  10. Booking too late. Accessible cabins book out first. Start 9+ months ahead.
